Notebook 0.2 - Goals and assumptions of this calibration

Goals of these notebooks¶

The goal of these notebooks is to calibrate 3 tree species for a study landscape in Quebec, Canada.

I limit things to 6 tree species as these notebooks are a proof of concept that will be used, if successful, for further calibration of more species in different Canadian landscapes.

The study landscape used here in Quebec, Canada, corresponds to a part of the territory of the Atikamekw first nation community of Manawan. This territory has been used before for previous LANDIS-II simulation, which will make comparisons to previous parameterization with Biomass Succession easier.

Here is a map showing the extent of the study landscape in space :

Assumptions¶

As per Reese et al., 2024 and other works, it is very important to document the assumptions (untested hypothesis) behind the choices done during the calibration.

Here, I catalog the different assumptions done throughout the calibration process. There is no need for you to learn or understand them all for now, as they will make sense while reading the rest of the notebooks.

  • Species parameters of different geographic source (e.g. derived from measurements made from trees outside of the study landscape in Quebec, or from trees of different geographic origin) can be used and are used in this calibration. We assume that despite the different geographic origin of these parameters, which might represent the behavior/biology of different variants or phenotypes, the calibration process will still allow us to tweak the values of the most important parameters influencing the growth of the tree species in the study landscape so as to properly replicate the behavior/physiology of the tree variants/phenotypes inside the study landscape. See 3.Initial_Species_Parameters.ipynb.

  • The raster data from the National Forest Inventory, which represents the biomass of a given tree species inside pixels of a certain forest age, can be used through a space-for-time approach as way to estimate the growth curves for this tree species. In particular, we assume that the maximum biomass reached for a certain species at a certain age, visible through the combination of the many data points that each pixel represent, is a good estimate of the growth peak that can be achieved by the tree species. See 4.Getting_the_empirical_data.ipynb.

  • The longevity values derived from Loehle (1988) are a good estimate of the longevity of the tree species in our study area, despite the fact that the longevity of trees can change depending on their place of growth. See 4.Getting_the_empirical_data.ipynb.

  • While species’ tolerance to shade, drought, watterlogging, temperature (as described in the TRY database) can change depending on the variant/genotype inside a species, we assume that the relative difference between species will remain the same (e.g. if tree species A has a shade tolerance of 1 in the TRY database and tree species B has a shade tolerance of 3, then tree species A will remain much more shade intolerance than B whatever the landscape considered).
