Phase 1 🌳 : Calibration of species in optimal conditions (monoculture + mesic soil + average and unchanging climate)

Goal of this first calibration step¶

The goal of this first calibration phase, as recommanded by Gustafson and Miranda (2023), is to calibrate the growth and mortality of a given tree species in “ideal” conditions. This means simulating a monoculture (no light competition with another species) on a mesic soil that retains water correctly (no drought, but no watterlogging either) and with a very mild, average historical climate (no extremes, especially in precipitations and temperatures). What I’ve attempted here is to automatize the advice and tips of Gustafson and Miranda (2023) which can be complex.

This first calibration phase is really the only one taking place in this current version of the notebook, even though we might do some adjustments based on the results of the competition between the species (see Notebook 7). I had planned to calibrate the drought/waterlogging and temperature parameters, but have found it to be a quite complex endeavour for which we didn’t have the time yet. See Notebook 100 for indications of possible future inprovments.

In this first step, we can isolate the effect of the many parameters and factor that influence tree growth, and focus on the few parameters that should (on a theoritical level) influence tree growth and mortality in ideal conditions :

  • FracFol

  • FolN

  • TOWood

  • TORoot

  • SLWmax

  • FracFol (pioneers species tend to have relatively high values)

  • FolN (pioneers species tend to have relatively high values)

  • FracBelowG

  • Some others (see code sections and functions bellow)

There can be confusion between “optimal conditions in the study area” and “optimal conditions for the species”. The calibration tips of Gustafson and Miranda (2023) recommands the later; but so as we have produced growth curves as close as the study area as possible (see notebook 4) to avoid confusing variants and genotypes, I’m going here with using “optimal conditions in the study area”. What that means is that we’re going to use precipitations and temperatures that are from the study area, but use their historical values that we will average to produce a “mild” climate with no extremes and very regular conditions (see Notebook 5). We will then make sure that the species match the peak of biomass (age and height of the peak), which we obtained for data near the study landscape (see Notebook 4) in these mild but local climate conditions.

To give you a broad idea of what we’re going to do here (which I finally started to understand after a lot of experimentation), we’re going to make sure that in the absence of as much reduction factor of photosynthesis as we can (fTemp for temperatures, fWater for drought/waterlogging, fAge for longevity, etc.), the productivity of species is enough so that it reaches the biomass peak found in the empirical data. We’re going to adjust this productivity so that not only do we match the peak height and timing (age), but we do so with the right amount of foliage/LAI (to make sure that species of different shade tolerance have the right amount of LAI; see Notebook 4 as to where we get these LAI targets), and by making sure that the beginning of the growth curve is acceptable. Finally, we calibrate the senescence using the methodology of Brian Sturtevant’s team.

All of this is a jungling act between many parameters, because changing one parameter (e.g. FolN for the overall productivity of the cohort) will often change different things in return (e.g. changing both the peak age and the peak height and the LAI). That why we will do it in different “substeps” where we try to focus on one aspect of the curve at the time.

I’ve composed the algorithms of each substeps myself (e.g. “First, change FolN until the peak height is right; then, change SLWMax to change the LAI; etc.”), and then used AI models to transform them into python functions (which are located in functionsForCalibration.py). Their outputs can change a bit and not be completly uniform, but I made sure that each functions describes precisely what is going on (i.e. what parameter is being changed, what is the target, etc.).

Current limitations of this calibration methodology :¶

  • TOWood and TORoot are used as kind of “garbage bins” to adjust the height of the biomass peak without changing the speed/age/timing of the peak too much by throwing away a part of the biomass of the cohort at each timestep. It’s really hard to have empirical/realistic estimates of the values of these two parameter for real trees, and to be able to juggle all of the other parameters without dealing with a lot of interactions between their effects; because TOWood and TORoot are quite predictable in their effect, their are used a lot to alter the biomass peak height. I’ve talked with Eric Gustafson, and he told me that it’s surely quite an inevitable part of the model (that we need to have some parameters that allow us to adjust the rest). I quite agree with him.

  • The algorithms can sometimes get stuck into the wrong places of the parameter space despite the checks I’ve implementend. This is because all of the algorithms are quite simple, and usually change one or two parameters at a time until reaching a target. It worked quite well for me so far; but if you’re getting strange results or strange outputs, you should read the outputs to see what’s going on, and most likely adjust your initial parameter values (or the criterias/targets of the functions). One of the most common thing that happen is that increasing FracFol doesn’t increase the LAI of the cohort because we’ve put it in the situation where the cohort is starved for carbon; you can detect that by seeing that the wood biomass of the cohort is very low.

You can imagine that the algorithms trying to find the right combination of parameters for your targets is a bit like playing golf. Our goal is to reach a certain area of the parameter space, where the model gives us the behavior that we want. Moving the golf ball accross the parameter space is done by changing the values of our parameters. But it’s like the golf course (the parameter space) contains hazards, holes, bunkers, water surfaces, or other things where our golf ball can get trapped.

Once we’re in such traps, it can be difficult or even impossible to get out by simply pushing our ball around as we’ve been doing so far. The algorithms I’ve designed here (with a lot of trial and error) are made to avoid these situations; but I’m certain that such situations will arise, especially because the parameter space of PnET-Succession is very big and complex. Also, keep in mind that sometimes, there simply isn’t any “end” to our golf course, because the target we’re trying to reach (or the combination of targets) is just impossible for the model to do with the given constraints (i.e. bounds on the parameters values, or simply the functioning of the model). In these cases, the algorithms are designed to return either a warning allowing you to manually change the parameters or reconsider your targets; or to return the best parameters that it found (closest to what you wanted).

  • One of the recurring problems is at the beginning of the growth of the cohort; sometimes, it’s quite difficult to make a cohort grow fast enough to reach its targeted biomass peak age, without making it grow too much, and vice-versa. When this happens, the algorithms are stretching the models into strange locations of the parameter spaces where you will start to see things like “bumps” in the LAI of the cohorts near its peak, etc. This means that we’re pushing into parameters that make the model behave quite unrealistically. If you’re getting stuck into these kind of situations, it might be good to double check your growth curves (Notebook 4) to be certain of what you’re asking the model to do. You might also try to change the generic parameters - like MaintResp -, but be warned that they will affect all of the other species. Improvments of the algorithms (by editing the function used in the substep that is failing) is also possible.

Be certain of your weather data

Be certain to have good weather data. Because biomass accumulation is ultimately limited by water, your precipitation inputs should not be much greater than is typical for your study area, especially for the results of your simulation to match your empirical data better.

Empirical growth curves are less important than the relative differences between species

As recommanded by Gustafson and Miranda (2023), keep in mind that calibrating growth to strictly match empirical growth curves may cause competition behavior in the model that is unrealistic. As such, you may want to favor a shape that is somewhat consistent with other species that have the same growth strategy (e.g., pioneers v. shade-tolerants). This will be important for the next phase (calibrating competition outcomes).

Also keep in mind that the model equations may produce a curve shape that is somewhat different than your empirical curves (likely generated under specific conditions) and forcing the model to exactly match empirical curves may reduce its validity in landscape simulations. In particular, you should try to match the biomass accumulation where growth begins to plateau and produce relative rates of initial growth that reflect each species’ growth strategy (i.e., continuum of pioneer v. shade-tolerant). (Gustafson and Miranda (2023)).

The effect of longevity/age is disabled for the first substeps

For the first calibration substeps, you will see that the growth curves never go down, but will reach a plateau and stop.

This is because the first calibration substeps remove the effect of the longevity parameters (by putting the longevity of each species at 999 years), in order to remove the effect of age on growth.

The effect of age will be calibrated in section 1.4 with the calibration of senescence.

Code cells will display useful outputs

Each code cell will print detailed output about how the simulation is going on (evolution of the targets, parameter changes, etc.), allowing you to detect if one of the step is getting “stuck”, and why. When the calibration is finished for a species, the code will display a graph showing you the growth of the species wood biomass, LAI, and so on.

Sub-step 1.1 : Calibrating the peak¶

This substep is divided into two further sub-sub-steps, in order to follow the advice of Gustafson and Miranda (2023).

  • Sub-sub-step 1.1.1 : we adjust TOWood/TORoot for large adjustments of the growth curve peak. The objective is to match a peak that is a bit above empirical data (around 5-10%) to account for the fact that we are simulating in ideal conditions, while empirical data is not in ideal conditions. If we cannot adjust the peak sufficiently without going beyond the empirical limits of TOWood and TORoot (from previously published studies, see Notebook 4), an error message will be displayed with some diagnostics to help you re-work the initial parameter values for the species (e.g. maybe lower FolN, etc.), because your species might be growing way too much or not enough. In a sense, this substep acts as a “sanity check” for the rest; if the growth of your species is wildly different than what’s expected, this step will help you detect it and diagnose it. This will prevent you from wasting time and getting the other algorithms stuck because your initial parameters were producing values that did not make sense.

  • Sub-sub-step 1.1.2 : we adjust the LAI via changes in FracFol (for large adjustments), SLWMax (for fine-tuning), and FrActWd (for its stability, if it is unstable). Then, we re-adjust TOWood and TORoot to re-adjust the biomass peak. If we go beyond the empirical limits of TOWood and TORoot (from previously published studies, see Notebook 4), we will adjust FolN instead. If we still cannot adjust the biomass peak without going beyond the empirical limits of FolN, then an error message will appear. Once the biomass peak is re-adjusted, the algorithm will check if the LAI has changed too much because of this, and will attempt to re-calibrate the LAI, and then re-calibrate the peak once again. There’s a limit of 3 iterations of this bigger loop to try to make the LAI and the biomass peak converge. If they don’t, an error message will display.

These two sub-sub-steps use the biomass peak height and LAI targets defined in Notebook 4.

Exceptions in bypassing parameter bounds/limits

Some species can have more important values of TOWood and TORoot, bypassing previously published values. This is the case for the Red Maple (Acer rubrum), which is sometimes considered to have an important self-thinning at the tree scale and at the cohort scale according to discussions with Eric Gustafson. Sometimes, this increase in TOWood and TORoot is needed to make sure the species doesn’t grow too much despite correct initial parameters.

Sub-step 1.2 : Calibrating the biomass peak age¶

In this sub-step, we focus on calibrating the age at which the biomass peak of the cohort happens. This has a large influence on the beginning of the growth curve, which in turn will influence the competition between our species a lot.

The biomass peak age is derived from the empirical growth curves in Notebook 4.

This sub-step was the most complicated for which to create an algorithm, because of two reasons :

  • We are trying to calibrate the peak age, but still keep the right peak height (amount of biomass) and the right LAI. But altering one often alters the others, meaning with have to do regular corrections to the peak height and the LAI as we keep exploring the parameter space to get the right peak age.

  • It’s more difficult to move the peak age than it is to move the peak height and the LAI. There are four parameters we act on for this : MaxFracFol and FracFolShape together at once; FrActWd; and FolN. MaxFracFol/FracFolShape and FrActWd have quite complex effects, especially FrActWd; it’s quite easy to make the algorithm gets stuck because we made the cohort starved from carbon, and we can’t adjust its biomass peak height or LAI anymore.

In the end, the algorithm is in three phases :

  • First, we try to find a combo of MaxFracFol and FracFolShape that 1. will get us closer to the peak timing and 2. will avoid making our cohort “fragile”, meaning that it might create very irregular and strange dynamics of LAI because of how MaxFracFol works. This is done by increasing FracFolShape very quickly as MaxFracFol increases, as FracFolShape will reduce the effect of MaxFracFol to conditions where light is plentiful only as we increase it.

  • Secondly, the algorithm will adjust FrActWd to change the peak timing; but every two iterations of adjustments, we re-adjust the peak height (with TOWood/TOFol) and the LAI (with FracFol) so that they match what we had at the start. This is so that we don’t put ourselves in a problematic situation where the LAI and the peak height become so absurdly large (or small) that our cohort simply cannot grow correctly anymore, which then messes up the algorithm as it tries to keep adjusting FrAcWd. By constantly adjusting, we might take a bit more time, but we’re coming to it quite well in the end.

  • The third and final section is optional. We come to it only if we werent able to get the right peak age alongside the right peak biomass and LAI without reaching the upper or lower empirical bounds of FrActWd in the previous phase. In this last phase, we will change FolN to increase/decrease the overall productivity of the cohort, still adjusting the biomass peak with TOWood/TOFol and the LAI with FracFol regularly.

  • If the algorithm succeeds in having a peak age, peak biomass and LAI all in range of tolerance, everything’s great ! If not (it happens), the algorithm will return the “best” parameter set it has found. Sometimes, especially if your peak age is too low, the algorithm will not be able to reach it. In this kind of situations, it might be better to embrace something that is close to your empirical data, even if it’s not at your empirical data.

There are several arbitrary values here : the amount by which we do increments on the different parameters, how fast FracFolShape changes with MaxFracFol, etc. All of these I’ve found through trial and error, especially as I was trying to calibrate the red maple - which according to the NFI data has a quite a quick peak time at 50 years, requiring a lot of adjustment.

If the algorithm doesn’t work for your species, you might have to use the advice above to find the right parameter set manually, by exploring the parameter space by yourself.

About the tips of Eric Gustafson for the wood biomass at age 14

In the User Guide of PnET-Succession, Eric Gustafson recommands (by his own experience with the model) to make sure that the cohorts achieve a certain amount of wood biomass by their age 14, in order to distinguish the species in terms of their shade tolerance, to make sure that the more intolerant ones grow faster than the others :

Shade Tolerance CategoryTolerantSomewhat-tolerantIntermediateSomewhat intolerantIntolerant
Wood biomass at age 14 (g/m2)235360440560670

However, I was never able to reach these values. My species tended to grow way too slow for this. More generally, my biomass targets (biomass peak from the growth curves, see Notebook 4) were much lower than previously published studies. My current interpretation is this : Eric (and other researchers in the US) tended to use sample plot data or growth curves from the Forest Vegetation Simulator (which is based on sample plot data) to make the growth curves that served as references for this calibration step. However, I use the NFI data, which reports much lower values of biomass (in g/m2) than what sample plot data or the FVS give. It’s possible that it’s because the sample plot and FVS curves contain a lot of “extremes” because of the variability inherant to the sample plot data, while the NFI data is much more “averaged”, but most likely much closer to the truth too.

There’s also the question of transforming the estimates of biomass from sample plots from biomass (g) to biomass per surface (g/m2). There are always a lot of different hypothesis contained in this translation. The NFI data is already in g/m2, but sample plots and FVS curves often require to use hypothesis/assumptions to translate to biomass per surface. Again, I tend to think that the NFI data is closer to the mark, especially because it’s closer to what LANDIS-II does (LANDIS-II only does biomass per surface).

Sub-phase 1.3 (optional) : Accelerating early growth for species that grow too slowly¶

By calibrating the peak timing (sub-phase 2), it happens that some species do indeed get the right timing; but that they spents the first decades (sometimes up to 50 years) of their lives almost not growing at all.

This seems to be the result of an overall lack of productivity for these species in their early years which translate to a low LAI, and vice versa. This lack of productivity/foliage can be caused by different things at once, like the species having a low productivity to begin with (low FolN), a combination of parameters that affects the LAI which make it difficult for the species to accumulate it in its early stages (FracFol, SLWMax, etc.), temperatures, etc.

The problem is that if we try to change these parameters, we also affect the rest of the growth of the species; especially its biomass peak timing and peak height.

I’ve tweaked a lot with different parameters to try to find something that would allow us to accelerate the early growth, while not moving the rest of the growth curve too much. It’s quite difficult, especially if we need to really increase the early growth of species. The best parameters I’ve found are MaxFracFol/FracFolShape ; and MaxFolN/FolNShape. These are made to affect only the early growth; MaxFracFol and MaxFolN increase FracFol and FolN respectively, while FracFolShape and FolNShape reduce the effect of MaxFracFol and MaxFolN as the canopy closes (as fRad diminishes). These parameters have been added to the PnET-Succession code by Eric Gustafson for this exact goal (influencing the early growth but not the rest of the curve as much as possible). So here, we increase these parameters to make the early growth faster; and because we will change the rest of the curve anyway, we correct the LAI with SLWmax, we correct the peak timing with FolN, and then we correct the peak height with TOWood and TORoot again. It’s really a game of jungling at this point. Hopefully, this is the last complex step.

There remains the question of the target of early growth; how much do we want to increase the early growth of the species ? When do we stop ? I pondered about using another target, that we would call the “Biomass reached at 50% of the peak timing”. We could extract this value from the NFI data and GAMs in notebook 4. But after trying to deal with this, I realize that for the Black spruce (PICE.MAR, which had a slower growth than necessary and which was the motivation for this step) demanded too much adjustments to reach the right value of its biomass reached at 50% of the peak timing (as derived from NFI data). The model simply wouldn’t go there without creating other big issues and pushing the parameters to extra high values. I’ve got some hypothesis as to why this might be; it could be issues with the NFI data which overestimate early growth, or the fact that the parameters in the current PnET-Succession equation are too static and should change with the age of the cohort (i.e. the allocation of photosynthesis to the different part of the trees, or its turnover of wood and leaves, etc., are not the same when the tree is young versus when it gets old).

So, I went with a simple rule that worked well in the case of the Black spruce : the biomass reached at 50% of the peak timing should be at least 30% of the biomass peak height. That’s what this calibration step tries to ensure. For the rest, I leave my trust in the hands of the PnET model.

This step is simpler than others and might get stuck

The calibration algorithm used here is a bit simpler than the others because there are more parameters, and because I was running out of time. It might get stuck and overshoot its targets at some points. Be careful with it !

This step will be skipped for most species

If the early growth of the species is already good enough for a given species, this step will simply be skipped.

Sub-phase 1.4 : Senescence¶

Focusing on :

  • PsnAgeRed

  • Longevity (LANDIS-II Core species parameter; is outside of PnET Succession)

The last phase of the curve to calibrate is the senescence phase, where the tree cohort grows older. At it grows, its maintenance cost (in carbon) will grow; but its photosynthesis will be reduced through a reduction factor, and will completly stop as the cohort reaches the “longevity” core species parameter for its species. This is how tree cohort die of old age in PnET-Succession : by becoming progressively unable to sustain their maintenance costs through photosynthesis.

As such, there are two targets that are being calibrated here : 1) the age where photosynthesis will start declining because of age, and 2) the age where the photosynthesis reduction factor will become so low that the cohort will die by lack of ressources. These two targets are shown on the following figure :

The first target (initiation of decline) is defined in PnET with the parameter PsnAgeRed, which controls when the photosynthesis reduction factor fAge starts declining, and how fast.

The second target (age of cohort death) is influenced by the start of the decline (first target), but also with the “longevity” parameter given in the “Core” species parameter file (e.g. species.txt in /SimulationFiles/\PnETGitHub_OneCellSim_v8). When the age of the cohort reaches the “longevity” parammeter, the photosynthesis reduction factor fAge will reach 0 (no more photosynthesis takes place). Of course, the cohort will most likely die before it reaches 0 because its photosynthesis will have been too low for too much time, which will have depleted its ressources before fAge reaches 0. As such, we will try to input a “longevity” parameter value in the species parameter file that will kill the cohort at the age we want (e.g. that correspond to empirical data) in interaction with your first target. Note again that the cohort will very likely die before the longevity parameter.

As to where we can get estimates of these two targets (initation of decline and age of cohort death) for our species, this gets a bit complex. I used (see Notebook 4) the methodology of Brian Sturtevant’s team, which define the initiation of decline as the “Typical longevity” of a tree species as published by Loehle (1988), and the age of death of the “Maximal longevity” available in the same study. However, other sources are possible; see Notebook 4 for more.
